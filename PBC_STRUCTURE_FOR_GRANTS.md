## Executive Summary

**TELOS will be organized as a Delaware Public Benefit Corporation (PBC)** upon grant funding, with **TelosLabs LLC** as the commercial licensing subsidiary.

- Tax benefits similar to nonprofit (mission-driven)

## Why Public Benefit Corporation?

### 1. Mission Protection

**Problem**: Traditional C-Corps prioritize shareholder profit
**Solution**: PBC legally mandates public benefit alongside profit

**Delaware PBC Law (8 Del. C. § 361-368)**:
- Directors must balance profit AND public benefit
- Cannot be sued for prioritizing mission over short-term profit
- Annual benefit report required (transparency)

**For TELOS**:
- AI safety mission protected legally
- Can reject profitable but harmful partnerships
- Grant committees see long-term commitment

### 2. Grant Eligibility

**PBC Can Receive**:
- ✅ NSF SBIR (small business grants)
- ✅ NIH SBIR (healthcare innovation)
- ✅ DARPA (defense research)
- ✅ Foundation grants (public benefit mission)
- ✅ Venture capital (commercial potential)

**Unlike**:
- 501(c)(3) nonprofit: Can't receive equity investment
- Traditional C-Corp: Harder to justify public benefit grants

### 3. Tax Benefits (Similar to Nonprofit)

**Benefits**:
- Reduced corporate tax rates (if mission-focused)
- State tax exemptions (varies by state)
- Donor tax deductions (for contributions to benefit corporation)

**Unlike Nonprofit**:
- No IRS approval required (faster formation)
- Can distribute profits (motivates team)
- Can be acquired (exit strategy for investors)

>
> (b) **Healthcare and Medical Safety**: Reducing patient harm from AI-assisted medical decision-making through rigorous governance, adversarial validation, and regulatory compliance (HIPAA, FDA, EU AI Act).
>
> (c) **Open Science and Reproducibility**: Publishing research findings, open-sourcing core governance technology, and supporting independent validation by academic and regulatory institutions.
>
> (d) **Equitable Access**: Ensuring AI governance technology is accessible to public institutions, nonprofit organizations, and underserved communities, not solely Fortune 500 enterprises.
>
> The Corporation shall balance the pecuniary interests of stockholders, the best interests of those materially affected by the Corporation's conduct, and the specific public benefit identified above.

## Risk Mitigation

### Risk 1: Grants Don't Fund PBC

**Concern**: Some grant programs may not fund benefit corporations

**Mitigation**:
- NSF SBIR explicitly allows PBCs (small business definition)
- NIH SBIR allows PBCs (small business)
- DARPA allows PBCs (defense contractors)
- Foundation grants often prefer PBCs (mission-driven)

**Backup Plan**: Incorporate as C-Corp, convert to PBC later (Delaware allows easy conversion)

### Risk 2: Commercial Pressure vs Mission

**Concern**: Investors pressure to prioritize profit over AI safety

**Mitigation**:
- PBC charter legally requires balance
- Board includes independent benefit representatives
- Annual benefit report provides accountability
- Can reject profitable but harmful partnerships (legally protected)

### Risk 3: Higher Costs

**Concern**: PBC formation and reporting costs more than C-Corp

**Reality**:
- Formation cost: +$3K-5K vs C-Corp (one-time)
- Annual report: +40 hours/year (required anyway for grants)
- Legal review: +$2K/year (benefit analysis)

**Justification**: Mission protection worth $5K/year premium

## Questions Grant Reviewers May Ask

**Q: Why not just a regular C-Corporation?**
A: C-Corps prioritize shareholder profit by law. We need legal protection to prioritize AI safety even when investors push for short-term revenue. PBC provides this.

**Q: Why not a nonprofit 501(c)(3)?**
A: Nonprofits can't receive equity investment, limiting scaling potential. TELOS needs both grant funding AND commercial revenue to achieve sustainable impact. PBC enables both.

**Q: What happens if the company is acquired?**
A: PBC charter remains binding. Acquirer must continue public benefit mission. Directors can reject acquisition if it harms mission (legally protected). This protects against "mission drift" after exit.

**Q: How do you ensure 80% revenue to PBC actually happens?**
A: Licensing agreement between PBC (parent) and LLC (subsidiary) is legally binding. Annual audits verify compliance. PBC board (including independent directors) reviews quarterly.

**Q: Is this common?**
A: Yes. Notable PBCs include Patagonia, Kickstarter, Allbirds, Laureate Education. Over 10,000 PBCs incorporated in the US (growing 30% annually).
