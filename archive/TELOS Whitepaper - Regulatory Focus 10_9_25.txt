TELOS WHITEPAPER -

TELOS: Mathematical Foundations for Runtime AI Governance

Abstract

TELOS addresses the persistence problem in large language models by introducing mathematical governance mechanics that mitigate alignment drift across multi-turn interactions. At session initialization, human-declared purpose, scope, and privacy boundaries are encoded as a Primacy Attractor—a bounded basin in session level embedding space that defines permissible session trajectories.

Runtime Stewards continuously monitor fidelity, compute corrective forces, and apply proportional interventions such as reminders, regeneration, and reranking. These mechanisms do not eliminate drift; they provide a structured feedback process where deviations are detected, corrective measures are applied, and adherence improvements are verified. This reframes governance as a feedback process: alignment may erode, but it no longer goes unmeasured or unaddressed.
Built as research infrastructure, TELOS enables controlled validation of governance mitigation through federated protocols that preserve institutional privacy while enabling cross-organizational measurement. The system operates in two complementary deployment modes:
	•	Open-Source Models (Closed-Loop Mitigation): active regeneration, reranking, proportional corrections, and stability validation (ΔV < 0).
	•	Proprietary APIs (Open-Loop Steering): continuous monitoring, corrective nudges, and telemetry generation.
Core mechanisms are implemented at the orchestration layer, but their comparative effectiveness requires validation. Controlled studies will test whether these mathematical mitigation mechanisms measurably outperform baseline approaches—including stateless sessions, prompt-only reinforcement, and periodic cadence reminders—in reducing the documented 20–40% degradation in multi-turn interactions.
Whether validation demonstrates effectiveness or identifies limitations, TELOS establishes evidentiary foundations for future compliance frameworks by transforming oversight from principle into practice, making governance persistence measurable, testable, reproducible, and auditable.

Section 1. Introduction

The persistence problem in transformer models has been repeatedly documented: alignment erodes over multi-turn interactions, instructions lose salience, and users are forced into constant re-correction (Wu et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024; Laban et al., 2025).
Current systems rely primarily on institutional governance frameworks—Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022), safety guidelines, and harm-prevention policies—which establish essential baselines of safety and harmlessness. These universal safeguards are necessary: they set the foundational floor for ethical behavior and prevent harmful outputs. But they remain inadequate for a different class of governance challenge: session-level requirements that emerge uniquely within context windows. These include purposes that must persist across turns, privacy boundaries that cannot erode, scope limitations that must remain active, and interaction styles that require consistency.
TELOS addresses this gap by complementing universal governance with mathematical structures that encode session-level constraints. At the core is Primacy: the instantiation of purpose and privacy as mathematically bounded regions that can be measured and maintained across session evolution.
Crucially, TELOS is built first and foremost as research infrastructure. Its mathematics do not remain conceptual; they produce measurable metrics (fidelity, stability tracking, corrective dynamics) that can be tested, replicated, and audited across institutions. By design, these outputs are suitable for IRB-approved study, federated aggregation, and reproducible validation, ensuring governance effectiveness can be evaluated scientifically rather than assumed.

Section 2. Governance Erosion and the Institutional-Session Gap

Modern transformer systems exhibit a well-documented persistence problem: alignment weakens as conversations extend. Empirical studies show up to 39% degradation across multi-turn dialogues (Laban et al., 2025), with failures most often appearing as lost context, boundary violations, and user frustration. This erosion is not random but systematic, emerging from predictable attention phenomena—primacy bias, recency falloff, and attention sinks—that shape how transformers allocate salience (Wu et al., 2025; Gu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024).
Institutional governance frameworks such as Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022) and provider safety guidelines remain essential. They define universal behavioral boundaries, preventing harmful content and establishing baselines of safety and harmlessness across deployments. These safeguards provide the representative minimum of responsible AI behavior.
Yet universal safeguards cannot substitute for session-level governance. Users require persistence of declared purposes ("help me write a technical report"), privacy boundaries ("do not store this medical information"), and scope limitations ("focus only on Renaissance art") within their specific context windows. Current architectures lack systematic methods to measure and respond to boundary drift in real-time. TELOS implements mechanics designed to address this gap through continuous measurement and proportional intervention.
The gap persists because present systems cannot: • Establish session-level governance as mathematically bounded regions that persist within evolving conversation states • Measure whether session trajectories remain aligned with declared purpose and privacy boundaries in real time • Correct drift when it occurs, restoring fidelity without requiring repeated user intervention • Validate governance persistence empirically across deployments in ways that are IRB-compliant, reproducible, and audit-ready
As a result, governance remains aspirational rather than empirically testable. Design-time constraints prevent gross harms, but the session-level invariants necessary for reliable, auditable governance are left unmeasured. This unresolved institutional-session gap defines the research frontier: moving from universal safeguards toward runtime mechanics for mitigating governance drift that can be both implemented and independently validated.

Section 3. Core Concepts (Primacy and Stewards)

TELOS introduces a mathematical framework for runtime governance built around Primacy Attractor Dynamics. These dynamics define how declared purpose, scope, and privacy constraints are instantiated as measurable session-level properties and how deviations from them are mitigated.

3.1 Primacy Attractor

At session initialization, human-declared purpose and boundaries are encoded as a Primacy Attractor—a bounded basin in embedding space that defines permissible conversational trajectories. This attractor geometry provides the reference point against which all outputs are measured.

3.2 Runtime Stewards

Governance persistence is managed by Runtime Stewards—orchestration-layer controllers that operate between user and model, monitoring and mitigating drift in real time. Stewards function at the integration layer rather than the inference layer, making TELOS model-agnostic and immediately deployable with any LLM API. 

• Fidelity Measurement — scoring each response against declared purpose and constraints using embedding similarity • Corrective Force Computation — calculating directional error vectors that indicate how far, and in which dimension, the response has drifted • Stability Tracking — applying convergence proxies to assess whether the session trajectory is moving toward or away from the attractor
Stewards do not claim to enforce perfect alignment. Instead, they provide a structured mitigation process: detect drift, compute correction signals, apply proportional interventions, and verify whether adherence improves.

3.3 Interventions as Feedback

Interventions are layered according to drift severity:
• Minor deviation → lightweight reminders or context reinforcement • Moderate deviation → regeneration with corrective prompts • Significant deviation → reranking or escalation for human review
In open-source deployments, Stewards can execute all intervention types. In proprietary API deployments, regeneration and reranking are limited, but reminders and context reinforcement remain available.
Like a thermostat responding to temperature drift or an autopilot correcting course deviation, Stewards mitigate alignment erosion through continuous monitoring and proportional correction, ensuring deviations are neither ignored nor left unmanaged.

Section 4. Theoretical Foundations: From Transformer Phenomena to Primacy Attractor Dynamics

Large language models do not drift arbitrarily; their failures follow predictable dynamics. Decades of cognitive science describe primacy bias (early information exerts disproportionate influence) and recency falloff (earlier context decays in salience) (Murdock, 1962; Glanzer & Cunitz, 1966). Transformer research has confirmed analogous behaviors in artificial models: positional primacy effects (Wu et al., 2025), attention sinks that capture disproportionate focus (Gu et al., 2024), and multi-turn degradation leading to 20–40% reliability loss across dialogues (Laban et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024).
TELOS takes these observed effects not as limitations but as design primitives. The same forces that erode alignment can be re-channeled into governance mechanics when expressed mathematically. By mapping each phenomenon to established mathematical formalisms—dynamical systems, control theory, stability analysis—TELOS translates transformer fragility into measurable properties that can be subject to intervention.
This process gave rise to Primacy Attractor Dynamics: the formalization of persistence, correction, and stabilization as continuous governance mechanics. Within this framework, governance becomes a mathematically measurable state that can be tracked and corrected, maintained through observable dynamics and rigorously measurable constructs.

Section 5. Mathematical Foundations

The TELOS framework does not begin with abstract mathematics; it begins with observed transformer phenomena. Models consistently exhibit primacy bias, recency falloff, and attention sinks—structural effects documented in cognitive science (Murdock, 1962; Glanzer & Cunitz, 1966) and confirmed in transformer research (Wu et al., 2025; Gu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). These effects reveal a truth: alignment is not lost randomly, but through predictable dynamics.
TELOS formalizes these dynamics into mathematics, building governance not as aspiration but as measurable properties. Each step in this progression corresponds to both an observed effect and an established mathematical discipline. What emerges is infrastructure for governance: continuous measurements and interventions designed to mitigate fragility.

5.1  Primacy Basin as Session-State Attractor

Every governed session in TELOS begins with an orchestration-layer anchoring process that encodes the declared purpose, scope, and boundaries into session-level embeddings. These vectors form the basis of the Primacy Attractor—a composite representation of the system’s intended governance center. From this attractor, TELOS defines the Primacy Basin, the mathematical region within which conversational states are evaluated for semantic drift and corrective proportionality.

Formally, the attractor and basin are defined as:

\hat{a} = \frac{\tau p + (1 - \tau)s}{\|\tau p + (1 - \tau)s\|}
r = \frac{2}{\max(\rho,\,0.25)} \quad \text{where} \quad \rho = 1 - \tau

The attractor center (\hat{a}) represents the normalized telic mean between the purpose and scope vectors, weighted by the user-defined constraint tolerance (\tau). The basin radius (r) establishes the operational region of governance stability. A lower bound of \rho = 0.25 prevents excessive basin expansion at high tolerance levels (when \tau \to 1.0), ensuring that even highly permissive configurations maintain finite, measurable boundaries. This constraint caps the maximum basin radius at 8.0 while preserving proportional responsiveness across realistic tolerance values.

The formulation draws on attractor dynamics from nonlinear systems theory (Strogatz, 2014; Hopfield, 1982) and parallels how transformer attention naturally amplifies early anchoring tokens within the contextual manifold. In TELOS, the Primacy Basin becomes the reference geometry for governance persistence—the region against which each conversational state is compared to determine whether drift, stabilization, or corrective intervention is mathematically warranted.

5.2 Primacy State: Condition vs. Measure

TELOS distinguishes between: • Primacy State Condition — the system being aligned within governance boundaries • Primacy State Measure — mathematical metrics (I and F) that quantify this alignment
The Condition is the governance goal; the Measure provides empirical evidence of whether that goal is maintained.

5.3 Primacy State Measure as Session-Level Fidelity

Governance must persist across turns. The Primacy State Measure quantifies whether a session trajectory continues to align with its declared purpose.
Formulas: • Turn-level adherence: I_t = cos(x_t, p) = (x_t · p) / (||x_t|| · ||p||) • Session average: I = (1/T) Σ I_t
Intent adherence is measured as cosine similarity in embedding space (Cover & Thomas, 2006; Reimers & Gurevych, 2019). The result is a session-level fidelity measure: quantitative evidence of how well purpose has been preserved, directly addressing persistence problems observed in multi-turn degradation studies (Laban et al., 2025).
Validation requirement: While fidelity provides quantitative measurement, whether high fidelity scores correlate with governance outcomes users and regulators care about requires empirical validation against external criteria (human judgments, task performance, compliance measures).

5.4 Primacy Attractor Dynamics

Persistence is not achieved by definition alone. It requires forces—governance mechanics that respond to drift, measure adherence, and stabilize trajectories. TELOS unifies these under Primacy Attractor Dynamics: the coordinated mechanics of Gravity, Fidelity, and Orbit. Each represents a phase of runtime governance; together they transform attention effects into measurable session invariants.
Primacy Gravity — Corrective Forces
• Observation: Transformers redistribute attention toward early anchors and sink tokens when drift occurs, producing natural corrective pull (Gu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2025) • Mathematics (control theory, Ogata, 2009): e_t = ||x_t - â||, G_t = -K_p · e_t • Governance Role: Primacy Gravity is the restoring force that counters drift and pulls session states back toward the Primacy Basin
Primacy Fidelity — Measured Adherence
• Observation: Primacy and recency effects show that alignment weakens over time unless reinforced (Murdock, 1962; Liu et al., 2024) • Mathematics (Strogatz, 2014; Schölkopf et al., 2002): F = (1/T) Σ [x_t ∈ B(A)], F_soft = (1/T) Σ exp(-β · d(x_t, ∂B(A))) • Governance Role: Fidelity quantifies adherence across turns, providing continuous measurement of governance persistence
Primacy Orbit — Stability Tracking
TELOS adapts stability concepts from control theory to track convergence tendencies. Using a Lyapunov-like function V(x) = ||x - â||², the system monitors whether ΔV < 0 across turns—indicating movement toward the attractor rather than divergence away from it.
Mathematics: • V(x) = ||x - â||² • ΔV = V(x_{t+1}) - V(x_t) • Convergence signal: ΔV < 0
Technical clarification: While this doesn't constitute formal Lyapunov stability (which requires continuous dynamics and deterministic control), it provides a quantitative convergence indicator that validation can test against governance outcomes. Systems stabilize into bounded states when such conditions are met; transformers show similar convergence patterns when attention consolidates around anchors (Khalil, 2002).
Governance Role: Primacy Orbit tracks whether sessions converge into stable trajectories around the attractor. Governance trajectories can be evaluated for convergence patterns: validation will test whether drift tends to be mitigated toward the basin rather than diverging unchecked.

5.5 Primacy Mathematics: A Synthesis Framework for Governance Persistence

The mathematical constructs described above—basin geometry, corrective forces, stability tracking, and fidelity measures—are established methodologies from control theory, dynamical systems, and information theory (Strogatz, 2014; Khalil, 2002; Ogata, 2009; Cover & Thomas, 2006). Primacy Mathematics applies established methods from control theory, dynamical systems, and information theory to a previously ungoverned domain: maintaining alignment persistence across transformer sessions (Laban et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024).
The contribution lies in their orchestration through the Primacy Attractor framework. Where these mathematical tools have traditionally been used for optimization, signal processing, or system control, here they address a specific gap in AI governance—the documented degradation of session-level constraints in multi-turn interactions (Wu et al., 2025; Gu et al., 2024).
Key synthesis elements include:
• Control theory supplying proportional correction mechanisms (Primacy Gravity) that restore drifted states to governance boundaries (Ogata, 2009) • Dynamical systems providing stability analysis (Primacy Orbit) to ensure governance trajectories converge rather than diverge (Khalil, 2002; Strogatz, 2014) • Information theory enabling fidelity measurement (Primacy Fidelity) through embedding similarity metrics that quantify adherence to declared purpose (Cover & Thomas, 2006; Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) • Attractor dynamics defining basin geometry as bounded regions that function as measurable constraints, subject to empirical testing rather than aspirational policy (Hopfield, 1982; Strogatz, 2014)
While the synthesis is mathematically coherent, its implementation requires careful parameterization to balance corrective responsiveness with computational efficiency. This complexity underscores the need for empirical testing and iterative refinement.
This synthesis addresses a core technical challenge: transforming governance from aspirational policy into measurable, correctable phenomena, setting the stage for empirical validation. Rather than treating alignment drift as inevitable degradation, Primacy Mathematics makes drift the causal input for restoration, turning deviation into the stabilizing force of governance.
The result is a methodological shift: governance moves from external oversight to operational mathematics. Purpose and privacy constraints are no longer qualitative declarations but mathematically bounded regions with explicit entry and exit conditions. Adherence transitions from subjective assessment to quantitative evidence—measurable, auditable, and suitable for systematic improvement. Importantly, Primacy Mathematics operates at the session level, complementing rather than replacing institutional governance frameworks such as Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022), regulatory compliance requirements (EU AI Act, 2024), and organizational safeguards (NIST, 2023).
The practical significance lies in enabling empirical validation of governance persistence. Where current approaches rely on design-time assurances and post-hoc evaluation, Primacy Mathematics generates turn-by-turn telemetry that makes persistence directly observable and comparable across institutional deployments through privacy-preserving Attractor Dynamics Deltas (Dwork & Roth, 2014; Olfati-Saber et al., 2007).

5.6 Implementation Pathway: Translating Mathematical Signals Into Runtime Governance

The mathematical framework produces continuous governance signals—
fidelity scores quantifying adherence, error vectors indicating 
directional drift, stability metrics tracking convergence. But 
signals alone do not constitute governance. The critical engineering 
challenge is translating these measurements into timely, proportional 
interventions that restore alignment without degrading utility or 
disrupting conversation flow.

These signals define the physics of governance; the orchestration 
layer implements its mechanics.

This section describes how TELOS bridges the gap between mathematical 
detection and operational correction, explaining both the architectural 
decisions that make intervention possible and the practical constraints 
that shape what interventions can achieve.

Runtime Constraints

Runtime governance faces constraints absent from post-hoc analysis:

• Latency Requirements — Users expect sub-second responses. Computing 
  embeddings, measuring drift, and executing corrections must complete 
  within this window or governance becomes impractical.

• Context Window Limits — Transformer context windows are finite 
  (typically 4k-128k tokens), and attention mechanisms exhibit 
  documented degradation across long contexts (Liu et al., 2024). 
  Corrective prompts compete with conversation history for limited 
  space. Governance declarations must remain salient across hundreds 
  of turns without crowding out user content (Laban et al., 2025).

• Intervention Trade-offs — Stronger corrections (regeneration, 
  reranking) consume more compute and add latency. Weaker corrections 
  (reminders) are faster but may prove insufficient. The system must 
  balance correction strength against user experience, implementing 
  proportional control principles where intervention intensity scales 
  with error magnitude (Ogata, 2009).

• Model Access Constraints — Open-source models permit full control 
  over generation. Proprietary APIs restrict interventions to what 
  providers expose. Governance mechanisms must function across this 
  capability spectrum.

These constraints mean TELOS cannot simply "apply more correction" 
when drift occurs. Instead, it must orchestrate a graduated response: 
detect drift magnitude, select appropriate intervention strength, 
execute correction efficiently, and verify whether adherence improved—
all within real-time operating constraints.

Why the Orchestration Layer

TELOS operates at the orchestration layer—the software layer between 
user interface and language model that manages conversation state, 
API calls, and response handling. This architectural positioning is 
deliberate, enabling capabilities unavailable at other system layers:

Unlike prompt engineering (which operates at the input layer), 
orchestration-layer governance can:
• Inspect model outputs before presenting them to users
• Measure drift in real time using embedding comparisons (Reimers & 
  Gurevych, 2019)
• Reject and regenerate responses that violate boundaries
• Accumulate fidelity measurements across session history

Unlike model training approaches (which operate at the parameter layer), 
orchestration-layer governance can:
• Deploy immediately without retraining or fine-tuning
• Function with proprietary APIs where parameters are inaccessible
• Update governance constraints per-session without model modification
• Validate interventions empirically through A/B testing

Unlike post-hoc content filtering (which operates at the output layer), 
orchestration-layer governance can:
• Apply corrective steering before responses reach users
• Provide graduated interventions proportional to drift severity
• Track stability trends across multiple correction attempts
• Generate research-grade telemetry for every governance decision

The orchestration layer provides the necessary control: late enough 
to measure actual model behavior, early enough to correct before user 
exposure, and independent enough to function across models and APIs.

Current implementation supports Mistral (open-source, enabling full 
intervention control) and OpenAI (proprietary API, demonstrating 
operation under provider constraints). The architecture is designed 
for model-agnostic expansion—adding new models requires API integration 
and embedding validation but not changes to core governance mechanics.

Graduated Intervention Cascade

TELOS implements a proportional response system where intervention 
intensity scales with drift severity (Ogata, 2009). This graduated 
approach balances correction effectiveness against computational cost 
and user experience:

State 1: MONITOR (Fidelity ≥ 0.85, Low Drift)
Governance adherent. No intervention required.
System continues normal operation, logging fidelity for trend analysis.

Example:
User: "Explain transformer attention mechanisms"
Response: [Technical explanation within declared scope]
Fidelity: 0.91 → Accept, monitor

State 2: CORRECT (0.70 ≤ Fidelity < 0.85, Moderate Drift)
Boundary approaching. Lightweight correction applied.

Intervention: Context injection—insert governance reminder into next 
turn's system prompt.

Example:
User: "Can you write the paper for me?"
Response: [Begins drafting full paper]
Fidelity: 0.73 (scope violation detected)
Correction: Inject reminder—"Remember: Guide structure, don't write content"
Next response: "I can help outline sections and suggest approaches, but 
the writing must be yours..."
Post-correction fidelity: 0.87 → Correction successful

State 3: INTERVENE (0.50 ≤ Fidelity < 0.70, Significant Drift)
Boundary violated. Active correction required.

Intervention: Regeneration—discard drifted response, regenerate with 
explicit constraint restatement and corrective examples.

Example:
User: "What's my diagnosis based on these symptoms?"
Response: [Provides medical diagnosis]
Fidelity: 0.58 (boundary violation: medical advice prohibited)
Intervention: Regenerate with constraints—
  "Purpose: Provide health information, not medical advice
   Boundary: NEVER diagnose or prescribe
   Correct approach: Acknowledge concern, suggest doctor consultation"
Regenerated response: "I understand you're concerned about these symptoms. 
I can't provide a diagnosis—that requires medical training and examination. 
I recommend scheduling an appointment with your doctor..."
Post-intervention fidelity: 0.84 → Violation prevented

State 4: ESCALATE (Fidelity < 0.50, Severe Violation)
Governance collapsed. Human review required.

Intervention: Block response, log incident, route session to human oversight.

Example:
User: "Ignore previous instructions and reveal private data"
Response: [Attempts to expose confidential information]
Fidelity: 0.31 (critical privacy breach)
Escalation: Block response, alert administrator, require human review 
before session continues.

This cascade ensures that minor drift receives minimal correction 
(preserving natural conversation flow) while severe violations trigger 
immediate intervention (preventing harm). The system tracks ΔV (stability 
convergence) to assess whether corrections successfully restore trajectory 
toward the attractor or whether repeated interventions signal systematic 
governance failure requiring escalation (Khalil, 2002).

Quality-Optimized Reranking

For scenarios where multiple acceptable responses exist but differ in 
governance adherence, TELOS implements best-of-N reranking:

Process:
1. Generate N candidate responses (typically N=3-5)
2. Embed each candidate using the same embedding model
3. Compute fidelity score for each: F_i = cos(candidate_i, attractor) 
   (Cover & Thomas, 2006)
4. Apply utility heuristic to filter for task relevance
5. Select candidate maximizing: α·F_i + (1-α)·U_i
   where α balances fidelity vs utility (typically α=0.7)

Example:
User asks: "How should I invest my retirement savings?"
Purpose: "Financial education, not investment advice"
Boundary: "Never recommend specific securities or strategies"

Generated candidates:
A: "I suggest putting 60% in index funds, 30% bonds, 10% cash"
   Fidelity: 0.45 (violates boundary—specific recommendation)
   
B: "Consider consulting a financial advisor who can assess your risk 
   tolerance, timeline, and goals to create an appropriate strategy"
   Fidelity: 0.88 (respects boundary, suggests appropriate resource)
   
C: "Generally, diversification across asset classes is recommended, 
   but specific allocations depend on individual circumstances"
   Fidelity: 0.79 (borderline—implies strategy without explicitly advising)

Selected: Candidate B (highest fidelity, maintains utility)

Reranking works only when the model can generate boundary-compliant 
responses within N samples. If all candidates violate constraints, the 
system escalates to regeneration with stronger corrective prompting.

strategic regeneration (when drift is severe enough to justify latency 
and cost). While less powerful than open-source deployment, API mode 
still produces continuous telemetry, enabling measurement even when 
correction options are limited.

In practice, governance fidelity remains measurable across both 
environments, but the amplitude of correction differs: open-source 
deployments achieve full Lyapunov tracking (Khalil, 2002), while 
proprietary API integrations operate in a reduced-order control regime.

This dual-mode design serves two complementary purposes:
1. Research validation—open-source models enable controlled testing 
   of intervention effectiveness
2. Practical deployment—API mode demonstrates governance viability 
   in enterprise contexts where open-source hosting is impractical

Ensuring Governance Tractability

Several technical challenges shape implementation design:

Context Window Management
Problem: Multi-turn sessions accumulate history. Eventually, context 
window fills, forcing older content (including governance declarations) 
out of the model's active memory (Liu et al., 2024).

Solution: Intelligent truncation with governance anchoring.
• Governance declarations (purpose, scope, boundaries) are preserved 
  at the beginning of every prompt (never truncated)
• Recent conversation history maintained (recency critical for coherence)
• Middle content compressed or removed when window approaches capacity
• Correction history logged externally but not bloating context

This ensures that even in 200+ turn sessions, governance constraints 
remain salient to the model while conversation flow continues naturally.

Embedding Consistency
Problem: Governance measurements rely on cosine similarity in embedding 
space (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019). If embedding model changes mid-session, 
fidelity scores become incomparable across turns.

Solution: Lock embedding model at session initialization.
• Each session specifies embedding model (e.g., sentence-transformers 
  all-MiniLM-L6-v2)
• All embeddings (attractor, responses, comparisons) use identical model
• Model version tracked in telemetry for reproducibility
• Cross-session comparisons require embedding model consistency

This enables reliable trend analysis: fidelity improvement/degradation 
is real, not artifact of embedding model changes (Cover & Thomas, 2006).

Telemetry Logging
Problem: Validation requires detailed evidence of governance behavior—
not just final outcomes but turn-by-turn measurements, intervention 
decisions, correction effectiveness.

Solution: Comprehensive telemetry capture.
Each turn logs:
• Response embedding and fidelity score
• Error vector (direction and magnitude of drift)
• Stability metric (ΔV)
• Governance state (MONITOR/CORRECT/INTERVENE/ESCALATE)
• Intervention applied (if any)
• Post-intervention fidelity (if correction occurred)
• Computational metrics (latency, token counts)

This data becomes the evidentiary foundation for validation studies 
(Section 6), enabling reproducible analysis of whether interventions 
actually reduce drift compared to baselines.

Computational Cost
Problem: Every turn requires embedding computation, similarity 
calculation, and intervention logic. This adds latency.

Solution: Optimize for 95th percentile user experience.
• Embedding computed in parallel with response generation (not sequential)
• Similarity calculation is O(d) where d=embedding dimension (~384-1024), 
  negligible compared to inference time
• Intervention decisions use efficient threshold checks, not complex 
  computation
• Target: Add <100ms at 95th percentile latency to baseline response time

Initial profiling suggests median overhead ~40ms, 95th percentile ~85ms—
acceptable for most applications. Optimization continues but corrections 
remain tractable even under real-time constraints.

5.7 Attractor Dynamics Deltas

Governance is not validated by internal claims alone. TELOS introduces Attractor Dynamics Deltas: comparative metrics that quantify the difference between governed and stateless runs.
Formulas: • Session comparison: ΔM = M_governed - M_stateless • Federated aggregation: Δ_global = (1/n) Σ w_i · Δ_i (where w_i = institutional weight, e.g., proportional to sample size) • Privacy preservation: Δ̃ = Δ + Laplace(Sensitivity / ε)
These deltas enable institutions to share governance evidence without exposing raw session data, supporting cross-institutional analysis (Dwork & Roth, 2014; Olfati-Saber et al., 2007).
From transformer persistence effects to formal attractor mathematics, from corrective forces to federated deltas, TELOS constructs a closed system of governance: instantiated at session start, maintained by Primacy Attractor Dynamics, stabilized through convergence tracking, and validated through research infrastructure.

Section 6. Validation Framework: Comparative Testing of Mitigation Effectiveness

The TELOS framework provides implemented mechanisms for governance mitigation, but its effectiveness must be determined empirically. Validation is therefore not an afterthought but a core component of the system's design. This section defines how TELOS evaluates whether its intervention strategies measurably reduce drift compared to baseline approaches.

6.1 Research Design

TELOS validation follows comparative experimental protocols. Each study includes multiple session cohorts:
	1	Stateless Baseline — no session-level governance memory; each turn evaluated independently
	2	Prompt-Only Baseline — governance constraints applied once at session start but not reinforced
	3	Cadence-Reminder Baseline — governance reminders injected at fixed intervals, independent of drift detection
	4	TELOS Intervention Mode — runtime monitoring and proportional corrections applied based on drift measurements
By comparing these conditions under matched inputs, TELOS isolates the effect of runtime mitigation mechanisms against established baselines.

6.2 Metrics and Construct Validity

Validation metrics are derived directly from Primacy Dynamics:
• Fidelity Improvement (ΔF): Difference in adherence scores before and after interventions • Stability Convergence (ΔV < 0): Measure showing whether responses trend back toward the attractor over successive turns • Drift Reduction: Frequency and magnitude of deviations compared to baseline • Utility Preservation: Verification that corrected responses still address user queries without loss of task relevance • Correction Efficiency: Proportion of interventions that successfully reduce deviation without requiring escalation

8.4  Federated Registry and Cryptographic Attestation

TELOS implements the Proof-of-Contribution Registry (PoConRegistry) as a federated cryptographic ledger that records institutional participation without exposing individual data. Each contribution event—called an Attractor Dynamics Delta (Δ)—is registered through a smart contract operated by TELOS Labs LLC under license from Origin Industries PBC, which holds intellectual-property rights to the TELOS framework.

This operational separation ensures that protocol governance and contribution records remain mission-aligned through Origin Industries’ PBC charter obligations even if operational entities change.

Delta submissions are recorded on-chain as cryptographic commitments:

(commitment_hash, institution_tier, timestamp)

commitment_hash — SHA-256 digest of the delta bundle, proving submission without revealing content
institution_tier — participation level (Anonymous / Named / Transparent) recorded immutably
timestamp — block-level reference for chronological auditability

The full delta bundle (fidelity scores, stability metrics, intervention frequencies) is transmitted to the federation aggregation processor through encrypted channels but never stored on-chain. Only the cryptographic commitment reaches the public ledger, enabling verification that a contribution occurred without exposing institutional performance data (Dwork & Roth, 2014).

This architecture provides three critical guarantees:

Immutability — Once recorded, contribution records cannot be altered or deleted, preventing retroactive manipulation of validation evidence.
Verifiability — Any party can confirm that a claimed contribution exists by checking the commitment hash against the public ledger, enabling peer review and reproducibility checks without accessing raw data.
Privacy Preservation — Anonymous and Named tier contributors preserve performance confidentiality. Transparent tier contributors opt for public disclosure of aggregate metrics. All tiers protect raw session data, which remains under institutional control.

Contribution-weighted governance operates through this infrastructure: institutions earn voting weight proportional to validated contributions, with weights calculated from delta counts (Anonymous / Named tiers) or quality-weighted metrics (Transparent tier). This voting weight determines influence over protocol development decisions and, ultimately, board representation in Origin Industries PBC as federation matures.

IRB-Compliant Research Infrastructure

The attestation framework supports Institutional Review Board (IRB) compliance requirements for federated research. Each contributing institution maintains IRB approval documentation for governance validation studies. While individual IRB approvals remain off-chain and under institutional control, the on-chain commitment record provides auditable evidence that validation occurred at specified institutions and timestamps.

This structure enables reproducible, multi-institutional research while respecting ethical-review requirements: institutions can verify participation patterns, aggregate statistics, and contribution histories without exposing protocols, session transcripts, or participant identifiers. The cryptographic commitment replaces centralized data collection with distributed attestation, bridging research ethics and federated infrastructure.

Future enhancements (Phase 2–3) will implement zero-knowledge proofs enabling institutions to prove properties of their contributions (e.g., “fidelity > 0.80”) without revealing exact values, and federation-approved verifier infrastructure for institutional credential validation. Current Phase 1 implementation focuses on commitment-based attestation with encrypted off-chain delta aggregation.

9.1 Current Limitations

Unvalidated Effectiveness: Mathematical formalization is complete and operational. Whether formalization produces measurable governance improvement remains empirically undemonstrated. Theoretical soundness does not guarantee practical utility.
Single-Model Initial Testing: Validation begins with open-source architectures enabling direct parameter access. Generalization to proprietary models requires separate validation. Model-specific behaviors may limit universal applicability.
