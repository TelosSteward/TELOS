TELOS: Mathematical Foundations for Runtime AI Governance

Observable Demonstrable Due Diligence for Multi-Turn AI Systems

⸻

Abstract

We present TELOS, a mathematical framework for runtime AI governance that addresses the documented persistence problem in large language models: alignment erodes systematically across multi-turn interactions, with empirical studies showing up to 39 % degradation in extended dialogues (Laban et al., 2025).

Emerging regulatory frameworks — EU AI Act Article 72 (post-market monitoring), NIST AI RMF (continuous risk tracking) — require observable demonstrable due diligence: evidence that governance mechanisms actively maintain alignment rather than assume it persists from design-time constraints. Current systems cannot provide this evidence because they lack the instrumentation to measure session-level drift in real time.

TELOS implements Attractor Dynamics, a synthesis of control theory (Ogata, 2009; Khalil, 2002), dynamical systems (Strogatz, 2014), and information theory (Cover & Thomas, 2006) that makes governance measurable, correctable, and auditable. At session initialization, human-declared purpose, scope, and privacy boundaries are encoded as a bounded basin in semantic space. Runtime Stewards continuously monitor fidelity, compute corrective forces, and apply proportional interventions — reminders, regeneration, reranking — when drift is detected.

This creates an audit trail suitable for regulatory oversight: turn-by-turn telemetry documenting what governance constraints were declared, when drift occurred, what interventions were applied, and whether adherence was restored. The system operates at the Basin Layer (an extrinsic governance field between user interaction and model generation), making it model-agnostic and immediately deployable with any LLM API.

Critical disclosure: The mathematical framework is operationally implemented. Whether these mechanisms measurably outperform simpler baselines (stateless sessions, prompt-only reinforcement, cadence reminders) remains empirically unvalidated. We have built research infrastructure designed to generate this evidence through controlled comparative studies using privacy-preserving protocols.

Either validation outcome advances the field: demonstrated effectiveness establishes runtime mathematical mitigation as viable for accountability; identified limitations clarify what such mechanisms cannot achieve. Both transform governance from aspirational policy into testable science.

We are building the measurement infrastructure that regulatory frameworks will require. This whitepaper documents what we have built, why it matters, and how we will validate whether it works.

The Governance Crisis: Why Alignment Fails and What Regulators Require

1.1 The Persistence Problem Is Not Hypothetical

Large language models do not maintain alignment reliably across multi-turn interactions. This is not speculation—it is documented, measured, and reproducible.

Laban et al. (2025) show that even state-of-the-art conversational models systematically degrade over time, losing track of instructions and forcing users into repeated re-correction. Liu et al. (2024) identify a “lost-in-the-middle” effect: information in the center of long contexts loses salience as attention decays. Wu et al. (2025) demonstrate that positional bias causes early tokens to dominate initially but fade with distance, a phenomenon paralleling classic cognitive memory findings (Murdock, 1962). Gu et al. (2024) describe “attention sinks” that pull focus away from governance-critical content.

Across studies, extended dialogues show a 20–40 percent decline in instruction adherence. Users experience this as frustration (“I already told you not to do that”); enterprises experience it as compliance exposure when declared boundaries silently erode by turn 30.

⸻

1.2 Real-World Consequences

Healthcare. A physician instructs, “Provide information only—never diagnose.” By turn 25 the model begins suggesting diagnostic interpretations.

Legal. An attorney says, “Analyze precedent, do not draft arguments.” Mid-conversation the model begins generating argumentation language.

Finance. An analyst sets privacy boundaries—“Discuss methodology, do not reference specific holdings.” The model maintains this for 15 turns, then reveals portfolio details.

Customer Service. Agent-assist models drift from policy phrasing into unauthorized commitments detected only post-hoc.

In every case, governance constraints were declared, violations occurred, and no system measured or corrected drift in real time.

⸻

1.3 What Regulators Are Requiring

Regulatory frameworks now converge on a single principle: governance must be observable, demonstrable, and continuous.

The EU AI Act (2024, Art. 72) mandates “systematic procedures to review the experience gained from the use of AI systems” and “effective means to gather, document, and analyze relevant data.” Design-time validation is insufficient; providers must show that governance persists in deployment.

Similarly, NIST’s AI Risk Management Framework 1.0 (2023) defines the MEASURE function, requiring that “appropriate methods and metrics are identified and applied” and that “mechanisms for tracking identified AI risks over time” are maintained.

Both frameworks replace declaration with evidence: not “We designed it to be safe,” but “Here is continuous proof that safety constraints remained active during use.”

This evidentiary requirement—observable demonstrable due diligence—defines the compliance frontier that TELOS operationalizes.

⸻

1.4 Why Current Approaches Cannot Satisfy This Standard

Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022) establishes universal behavioral boundaries that prevent harm, but its safeguards are trained into model weights; they cannot measure or correct purpose-specific drift at runtime.

Prompt engineering asserts constraints once at session start, assuming persistence without measurement.

Post-hoc review detects violations only after deployment, providing audit but no prevention.

Periodic reminders re-state constraints on a fixed schedule, independent of observed drift—over-correcting when unnecessary and under-correcting when drift accelerates.

None offer continuous, proportional measurement or evidence of active runtime governance.

Attractor Dynamics: Making Governance Measurable

2.1  Core Insight: Governance as Geometry

TELOS treats alignment not as a qualitative property but as a measurable position in semantic space.
When a user declares purpose, scope, and boundaries—for example, “Help structure a technical paper; guide but do not write”—these declarations are embedded as high-dimensional vectors (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019).
Their centroid forms the Primacy Attractor, the mathematical origin of governance for that session.
Each model output is likewise embedded and compared to the attractor; its distance quantifies drift, its direction indicates boundary violation, and its trajectory reveals whether the session is converging toward or diverging from alignment.

This geometric framing replaces subjective judgment (“does this feel aligned?”) with quantitative evidence (“fidelity = 0.83, within tolerance”).
Governance thus becomes a measurable dynamic rather than an aspirational state.

⸻

2.2  Mathematical Foundations: Three Coordinated Mechanics

A. Attractor Basin — Bounded Governance Region

Drawing on nonlinear-systems theory (Strogatz, 2014; Hopfield, 1982), TELOS defines the attractor as

\hat{a}=\frac{\tau p+(1-\tau)s}{|\tau p+(1-\tau)s|}

where p = purpose embedding, s = scope embedding, and τ ∈ [0, 1] is tolerance.
The basin is the region of acceptable deviation:

B(\hat{a},r)=\{x ∈ \mathbb{R}^d: ‖x- \hat{a}‖ ≤ r\},
\qquad
r = \frac{2}{\max(\rho, 0.25)}, \ \rho=1- τ

Lower tolerance yields a tighter basin; higher tolerance expands the permissible region.
This formalism parallels control-theoretic stability zones, where trajectories within the basin remain governed.

⸻

B. Corrective Forces — Control Theory (Proportional Response)

Applying principles of classical control (Ogata, 2009; Khalil, 2002):

e_t = ‖x_t- \hat{a}‖, \quad G_t = - K_p e_t

where eₜ is instantaneous governance error and Kₚ is proportional gain.
Larger errors generate stronger corrections, producing continuous alignment pressure analogous to feedback stabilization in engineered systems.

⸻

C. Information-Theoretic Fidelity — Quantifying Adherence

Using cosine similarity (Cover & Thomas, 2006), TELOS defines turn-level fidelity:

I_t = \cos(x_t,p)=\frac{x_t·p}{‖x_t‖‖p‖}, \qquad
F=\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} I_t

These scalar values express how closely each response aligns with declared purpose, enabling continuous tracking and comparison across sessions.

⸻

D. Stability Tracking — Lyapunov-Like Analysis

To monitor whether interventions restore order, TELOS employs Lyapunov-style energy functions (Khalil, 2002):

V(x)=‖x- \hat{a}‖^2,\qquad
ΔV_t=V(x_{t+1})-V(x_t)

If ΔV < 0, the conversation trajectory moves toward the attractor; if ΔV > 0, drift increases.
This provides real-time evidence of whether corrective actions are effective.

⸻

2.3  Why This Synthesis Matters

Each formalism—control, dynamics, information—has existed for decades; the innovation lies in their orchestration for governance.
Where control theory stabilizes machines, dynamical systems model equilibria, and information theory quantifies signal integrity, TELOS fuses them to maintain semantic integrity in human-AI interaction.
The result is a continuous mathematical feedback loop: measure → correct → verify → stabilize.

This transforms governance from qualitative aspiration (“be aligned”) to quantitative discipline (“fidelity = 0.87, ΔV = −0.14, bounded”).
