## The Problem: Governance Erodes in Use

Large language models lose alignment over time. Studies show 20–40 percent degradation in adherence to declared purposes and boundaries during extended interactions (Laban et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2024). This “persistence problem” undermines compliance, safety, and trust.

Design-time safeguards like Constitutional AI prevent harmful content but cannot ensure persistence once conversations begin. Models must remain not only safe but purpose-aligned, preserving declared objectives, scope, and privacy boundaries through dynamic dialogue. Users experience drift as constant re-prompting; enterprises face compliance risk when violations go unnoticed; regulators lack real-time evidence that safeguards held.

⸻

## The TELOS Framework: Making Governance Measurable

TELOS reframes governance as a measurable dynamic process by orchestrating three established mathematical disciplines:

• **Control theory** – determines how and when to apply proportional corrections when drift is detected  
• **Dynamical systems theory** – defines attractors and basins, measurable regions of stability  
• **Information theory** – quantifies fidelity, continuous evidence of adherence to declared purpose

TELOS operates entirely at the orchestration layer—between user interface and model API. It requires no model retraining or internal access; the model remains a black box. At session start, declared purpose, scope, and privacy boundaries are embedded as vectors forming a Primacy Attractor, a reference geometry for acceptable behavior. Each response is vectorized and compared against this attractor to compute fidelity (adherence), gravity (corrective force), and orbit (stability).

When drift occurs, Runtime Stewards—orchestration-layer controllers—apply proportional interventions: reminders for minor drift, regeneration for moderate violations, and escalation for severe breaches. Each decision is logged as telemetry, creating verifiable audit trails of governance in operation.

This placement is deliberate: late enough to measure real behavior, early enough to correct before user exposure, and independent enough to work across both open-source and proprietary systems.

## Validation-First Approach

The empirical question: Does runtime mathematical mitigation reduce drift more effectively than existing baselines?

Comparative experiments will test four governance modes under identical prompts:

1. **Stateless** (no governance memory)
1. **Prompt-only**
1. **Cadence-reminder**
1. **TELOS intervention**

By holding inputs constant and varying only governance mode, we isolate whether continuous monitoring and proportional correction actually reduce drift.

All studies will be IRB-approved, pre-registered, and published regardless of outcome, using federated protocols that protect institutional data sovereignty. Success means showing that governance constraints hold more reliably over time and that user correction burden declines measurably.

## Why This Matters Beyond TELOS

Even negative findings advance the field by:

• Establishing governance persistence as measurable science, not assumption  
• Creating federated validation protocols for reproducible cross-institutional research  
• Providing the measurement primitives future compliance frameworks will require  
• Demonstrating what observable, demonstrable due diligence looks like in practice

Emerging standards—EU AI Act Article 72 and NIST AI RMF—demand continuous monitoring evidence rather than design-time assurances. Whether regulators adopt TELOS’s exact approach or not, they will need this class of measurement infrastructure.
